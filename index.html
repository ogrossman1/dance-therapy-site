Research findings
What the science says
Underlined text — hover for full citation
Parkinson's Disease
A study in the Journal of Rehabilitation Medicine examined whether the basal ganglia can be activated during rhythmic, metered movement such as tango dancingHackney & Earhart (2009). Effects of dance on movement control in Parkinson's disease: a comparison of Argentine tango and American ballroom. Journal of Rehabilitation Medicine, 41(6), 475-481. doi:10.2340/16501977-0362, potentially increasing motor control in patients with Parkinson's disease. The researchers compared the effects of tango, waltz/foxtrot, and no dance intervention on functional motor control. The study found that tango may target Parkinson's-related deficits more than waltz/foxtrot, but both forms appeared to help with balance and locomotion — supporting dance therapy as a beneficial intervention for Parkinson's patients. Another study examining exercise therapy and music in Parkinson's patients found that music-based movement (MbM) therapy shows promise for improving gait and gait-related activitiesde Dreu et al. (2012). Rehabilitation, exercise therapy and music in patients with Parkinson's disease. Parkinsonism & Related Disorders, 18(S1), S114-S119. along with walking ability, balance, and quality of life.
Alzheimer's & Memory
Age-related degradation in brain structure is linked to balance issues and cognitive impairment. Neuroplasticity, however, is understood to be preserved throughout a lifetime, and research on physical training in seniors suggests it can meaningfully increase hippocampal volume — a region essential for memory retention and spatial navigationRehfeld et al. (2017). Dancing or Fitness Sport? The Effects of Two Training Programs on Hippocampal Plasticity and Balance Abilities in Healthy Seniors. Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 11:305. doi:10.3389/fnhum.2017.00305, alongside improvements in aerobic fitness. That same study found dancers showed additional increases in the left dentate gyrus and right subiculumRehfeld et al. (2017). Dancing or Fitness Sport? The Effects of Two Training Programs on Hippocampal Plasticity and Balance Abilities in Healthy Seniors. Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 11:305. doi:10.3389/fnhum.2017.00305 — the dentate gyrusJonas & Lisman (2014). Structure, function, and plasticity of hippocampal dentate gyrus microcircuits. Frontiers in Neural Circuits, 8:107. doi:10.3389/fncir.2014.00107 being a hippocampal region tied to memory and information processing, and the subiculumO'Mara (2005). The subiculum: what it does, what it might do, and what neuroanatomy has yet to tell us. Journal of Anatomy, 207(3), 271-282. doi:10.1111/j.1469-7580.2005.00446.x tied to spatial navigation and stress response. A separate study synthesizing existing data on dance movement interventions in older adults with mild cognitive impairment, Alzheimer's disease, and dementia found that dance interventions had a significant effect on global cognition, memory, and balance, and significantly decreased depressionSystematic scoping review & meta-analysis (2023). Effectiveness of dance movement interventions in older adults with mild cognitive impairment, Alzheimer's disease, and dementia. ScienceDirect..
Mood & Anxiety in Hospital Patients
As mentioned in the Alzheimer's research above, dance interventions have also been associated with decreased depression levels. Beyond reducing depression, dance has been linked to increased overall mood. Recent research on exercise as a form of automatic emotion regulation has shown altered amygdala activation associated with mood volatilityScienceDirect (2025). Boosting your mood: How exercise and the amygdala dance together.. When researchers examined whether specific regions of the amygdala play distinct roles in the mood improvements caused by acute exercise, they found that particular amygdala regions were associated with reduced negative affect, increased self-esteem, reduced tension and anger, and increased vigor, respectivelyScienceDirect (2025). Boosting your mood: How exercise and the amygdala dance together.. Another study synthesized 41 controlled intervention studies and found that dance movement therapy decreases depression and anxiety while increasing quality of life and interpersonal and cognitive skillsKoch et al. (2019). Effects of dance movement therapy on health-related psychological outcomes. Frontiers in Psychology, 10, 1806. doi:10.3389/fpsyg.2019.01806. In summary, dance is essentially movement synchronized to music — and music itself has been shown to trigger dopamine release in the brain's reward circuitry, including the nucleus accumbens — a neurotransmitter directly tied to feelings of reward and pleasureSalimpoor, Benovoy, Larcher, Dagher & Zatorre (2011). Anatomically distinct dopamine release during anticipation and experience of peak emotion to music. Nature Neuroscience, 14(2), 257-262. doi:10.1038/nn.2726. Music is another core part of what makes dance so powerful and memorable.

Ballare Studio & research
Free routines. Real research.
Ballare Studio, our companion app and website, is more than a way to deliver free, clinician-approved routines — it's also the research arm behind everything on this page.
What it offers
Free, clinician-approved routines shown to improve symptoms in conditions like Parkinson's and Alzheimer's — safe for anyone to use with confidence, whether or not they have a diagnosis.
How you can contribute
Optional follow-up surveys let users report how they're doing before and during their routines — anonymized data that helps us learn which specific movements help most, for which conditions.
It's a loop: routines help people today, their feedback sharpens our understanding of dance therapy, and that understanding shapes better routines tomorrow.

About
About Ballare Studio & The Ballare Foundation
Ballare Studio is a free library of dance therapy routines, built for hospital patients, people with limited access to healthcare, and anyone looking to move more and feel better. Every routine is created with a licensed dance therapist and approved by a physical therapist, so you can move with confidence knowing what you're doing is safe.
Ballare Studio is the free program built and run by The Ballare Foundation, a nonprofit dedicated to researching and expanding access to dance therapy. When you use Ballare Studio, you can also choose to contribute to that research — helping us learn more about which movements help most, for which conditions.
The Ballare Foundation was founded by Olivia G. Grossman. Leads Ballare Studio's dance therapy program, designing every routine from 13 years of dance experience, training in a pre-professional dance program, performance experience with a professional dance company alongside seasoned professional dancers, and years spent teaching dancers ages 5 to 18. Also drives Ballare Foundation's research initiative, drawing on years spent studying how dance affects the brain and the broader neuroscience of the mind-body connection.
Our mission is to make the healing power of dance accessible to all who could benefit from it, and to build a greater understanding of why it works.

Privacy Policy
Effective date: [to be set at launch] · Last updated: [to be set at launch]
This Privacy Policy explains how The Ballare Foundation ("we," "us," "our") collects, uses, and protects information across both The Ballare Foundation website and the Ballare Studio app and website. By using either, you agree to the practices described here.
Information we collect
Account information: name, email address, password (stored securely by our authentication provider, never in plain text), date of birth, and mobility type when you create a Ballare Studio account.
Parent/guardian information: if the account holder is under 13, we collect a parent or guardian's name and email address to request and record their consent before the account is created.
Newsletter signups: your email address, if you choose to subscribe to updates from the footer of our site. You can unsubscribe at any time.
Research survey responses: your answers to optional baseline and follow-up check-in surveys, which we use to better understand how dance therapy affects the body.
Kit order information: your name and shipping address, collected only if you request a free exercise kit.
Usage data: which routines you complete and your progress over time.
Contact and partnership information: your name, organization, email, and message if you contact us or inquire about a partnership.
How we use your information
We use your information to provide and improve Ballare Studio, ship free exercise kits, send newsletter updates (only if you've subscribed), respond to inquiries, and — only with your participation — advance our research into how dance therapy affects the body. We do not sell your personal information.
Research use and anonymization
Survey responses used for research are anonymized and, where possible, analyzed and published in aggregate rather than tied to your identity. Participation in any research survey is always optional, and you can skip or stop at any time.
Children's privacy
Ballare Studio is used by patients of all ages, including minors. If the person signing up is under 13, we do not create an account or store any personal information until we have verifiable consent from a parent or guardian, in accordance with the Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA). While we wait for that consent, the child's information is held only temporarily and is deleted if consent is not given. Once approved, we record the parent or guardian's name and email alongside the account so they can contact us at any time to review, correct, or delete their child's information.
How we store and protect your information
Your information is stored using Supabase, a secure database and authentication provider. Passwords are never stored in plain text — they're handled by Supabase's authentication system using industry-standard encryption. Access to your data is limited by access controls restricting who can view it. While no system is completely immune to risk, we take reasonable technical and organizational measures to protect your information from unauthorized access, loss, or misuse.
Sharing your information
We do not sell or rent your personal information. We may share limited information with service providers who help us operate Ballare Studio (such as our database and email providers), and only to the extent needed for them to perform those services. We may also disclose information if required by law.
Your rights
You can request access to, correction of, or deletion of your personal information at any time by contacting us using the information below. You may also opt out of any research survey without affecting your access to routines or your account.
Changes to this policy
We may update this Privacy Policy from time to time. We will post any changes on this page and update the "last updated" date above.
Contact us
If you have questions about this Privacy Policy or your information, contact us at inquiries@ballarefoundation.org.
This draft covers the practices Ballare currently has in place. We recommend a legal review before publishing, given the collection of health-related survey data and information from minors.
